      In Nepal, inadequate water, sanitation and waste disposal facilities, (particularly in rural areas) as well as cultural interpretations of menstruation as polluting cause significant challenges for women and adolescent girls whilst menstruating. The Government of Nepal’s Child Friendly Schools Initiative includes child-gender-disabled WASH facilities as one of nine elements of quality education. In order to contribute to this initiative, and address the barriers to good menstrual hygiene, WaterAid and its partner Nepal Water for Health (NEWAH), are working with the Government of Nepal in the districts of Siraha, Udaypur and Sindhuli, to improve water, sanitation and hygiene (WASH) facilities in schools, and to develop the capacity and accountability of local stakeholders to facilitate confident menstrual hygiene management (MHM) in schools. The project is supported by funding from UK Aid. The overall aim of the project is to contribute to improving gender equality in schools by increasing access to MHM-friendly WASH facilities, sanitary materials, and girls’ confidence to hygienically manage menstruation at school.

1. Why?

Official statistics show high water access in the District (90%), however, many water facilities are non-functional or contaminated, and marginalised groups such as dalits (a designation  for  a  group  traditionally  regarded  as  ‘untouchable’)  have  much  lower access to them. In Siraha, less than 21% of the population has access to improved sanitation, meaning that most people practice ‘open defecation’ (going to the toilet in fields or open spaces). In one of the villages, access is as low as 4%. Open defecation denies people, especially women and children, dignity, and results in faecal contamination of water sources and the environment. This contributes to high prevalence of water-related diseases such as diarrhoea. Poor sanitation is reflected in poor hygiene practices, with low levels of hand-washing with soap, exacerbating the health problems linked to open defecation.

WaterAid and Partners will address the low sanitation coverage and access to clean drinking water of the target area. The proportion of Dalit community is significant in this district that has lowest coverage of sanitation facilities. The project aims to improve sanitation facilities in those communities. Along with this WAN and partners will carry out lobby and advocacy activities to ensure right to water and sanitation of marginalized communities of the district. WAN and partners will work to improve water, sanitation and hygiene status in school. 

2. What?

 WaterAid aims to transform the lives of poor communities in Siraha District, Nepal, by securing their human right to safe water and sanitation. Over three years, WaterAid will support 9,765 people to gain access to safe water, 30,874 people to access improved sanitation, and 75% of the targeted households to practice improved hygiene behaviours.

Under this project activities will be delivered to achieve following three outcomes:

Outcome 1: Increased access to safe water, improved sanitation and hygiene behaviours in targeted communities. Outcome 2: Marginalised communities enabled to manage their WASH services sustainably  and  demand  accountability  in  WASH  service  provision  from  local authorities. Outcome 3: Strengthened government capacity, resulting in increased accountability for provision of sustainable WASH services to the most marginalised communities.

The total expenditure requirement for the project is £672,034 (£500,000 from BIG and £172,034 from WaterAid).All the funding are secured.

Length of project is 36 months 

3. Where?

This project covers 15 wards of three municipalities(Goalbazar 7, 8; Mirchaiya 1-9 wards; Siraha 3,5,6 and 9) and one VDC-Karjanha for service delivery. Apart from this, it also covers 48 schools of 38 communities and 4 VDCs and 14 Municipal wards of  3 municipalities(Goalbazar, Mirchaiya  and Sirha)

WaterAid will pay particular attention to these key groups:

1.  Dalit communities, with special focus on dalit women: Making up 30% of the population in Siraha, dalits constitute one of the most marginalised communities who belong to the ultra-poor category economically and are subjected to various forms of social exclusion and discrimination, being regarded as ‘untouchable’. Dalit women  not  only  lack  access  to  WASH  facilities,  but  have  weak  political organisation which would enable them to speak up for their rights and engage with government bodies to realise their rights.

2.  School children, with special focus on girls: WaterAid will address children’s needs for safe WASH at school, especially the needs of girls. On average 249 children have to share just one latrine which means that the latrines are often unhygienic and do not allow schoolchildren to defecate with dignity.

3.  Wider community with special focus on children, women, elderly and people with disabilities: WaterAid will be inclusively targeting all households and encouraging communities to work together towards community-wide improved sanitation. In line with WaterAid’s equity and inclusion (E&I) approach the needs of the most marginalised sections of the target communities such as women, children, elderly and people with disabilities will be prioritised.

4. Who?

In this project WaterAid will work with four experienced local partners who each bring WASH and social development expertise that will contribute to achieving the project outcomes.

  NEWAH (Nepal Water for Health) has been supporting rural communities to improve WASH since 1992, and since being established has worked closely with WaterAid. It has pronounced expertise in water service management, community led total sanitation approach and hygiene behaviour change program at community level.

UEMS (Urban Environment Management Society) has been supporting urban communities since 2002. UEMS became WaterAid’s partner in 2004 and since then has  been  working  to  improve  access  to  safe  drinking  water  and  sanitation,  and promote hygiene practices in urban areas of Nepal. It has expertise in water and sanitation service delivery program in urban and semi urban setting.

FEDWASUN (Federation of Drinking Water and Sanitation Users Nepal) was established in 2003 as an umbrella network to support community-based WSUCs. WaterAid has worked with FEDWASUN since 2003, and in 2013 particularly focused on raising the advocacy issue of poor school WASH in 13 districts across Nepal. FEDWASUN has network of members across 52 of Nepal’s 75 districts to maximise the advocacy efforts of the project.

FEDO (Feminist Dalit Organisation) was established in 1994 and has been working with WaterAid since January 2014 when they became an advocacy partner. FEDO is right based organization having expertise in highlighting Dalit’s WASH needs to duty bearers, as equal citizens with equal rights, through advocacy events and meetings with V- and D-WASHCCs.

Overall management and coordination will be done by WaterAid Nepal. Assurance and systems support will be done from WaterAid’s UK head office

1. Why?

 

Access to sanitation is fundamental to human development and well-being, including the achievement of adequate nutrition, gender equality, education and the eradication of poverty. However, even with its critical role in human development and well-being, more than half of the urban population in Tanzania do not have access to improved toilets. In some urban communities, only one in eight persons uses a household toilet. This is particularly problematic to health in densely populated, unplanned urban settlements. Despite the Government of Tanzania concerted effort to achieve access to safe and adequate water and sanitation service, to-date there is a lack of focused attention to demonstrate how to realise universal access scenario in a location, as an example to inspire national scale up. It is generally believed that urban areas will increase in number, population and importance. Improving our understanding of how sustainable universal sanitation services can be provided in small towns or urban growth centres will remain high on the agenda, or even rise in the years to come.

This study therefore aims to contribute to answering the largely unanswered question in urban sanitation - how to identify which service delivery model to apply where and at what scale? The research project will to contribute to the sectors understanding of how to achieve sustainable universal access to sanitation services in Babati, Town, with a view to eventually up-scaling this lessons on sanitation in other urban areas of the country. Secondly, the study will demonstrate the conditions under which municipalities and citizens can co-produce and implement a town-wide sanitation plan that can deliver inclusive and sustainable sanitation services to all. 

2. What?

 This study focuses on answering the largely unanswered question in urban sanitation - how to identify which service delivery model to apply where and at what scale? The research project will to contribute to the sectors understanding of how to achieve sustainable universal access to sanitation services in Babati, Town, with a view to eventually up-scaling this lessons on sanitation in other urban areas of the country. Secondly, the study will demonstrate the conditions under which municipalities and citizens can co-produce and implement a town-wide sanitation plan that can deliver inclusive and sustainable sanitation services to all. Through cross-sectional study using both qualitative and quantitative methods will be applied to understand: 1) what motivates and drives people (households) to invest in and improve sanitation and hygiene practices in an urban context; 2) what drives institutions (government, donors, NGOs, authorities), and private sectors to invest and/or improve sanitation and hygiene practices in an urban context; 3) the main economics, social, political and institutional determinants of success for urban authorities to design and implement an inclusive sanitation and hygiene plan; and 4) the risks and potential factors (including public health hazards) that relates (drive) hygiene and sanitation behaviour change. In addition, the study will contribute to development of Babati town-wide sanitation and hygiene adaptive master plan that promotes inclusive, sustainable and affordable hygiene and sanitation services for all.

To achieve these the study will consist of five phases described in detail below.-

Phase 1: Inception– This phase will include procurement of resources and a small project launch workshop. This time will also be used to gaining-country ethics approval.

Phase 2: Data collection & diagnostic – This phase will include the secondary and primary data collecting, cleaning and analysis, as well as development of relevant learning notes, briefing papers and manuscripts. -

Phase 3: Participatory planning and scenario building – This phase will be working closely with all stakeholders to jointly reflect on the data collected and use this to identify key planning strategies, actions and goals, as well as build modules for a range of scenarios.-

Phase 4: Town-wide sanitation and hygiene plan – During this phase stakeholders will agree on the scenario(s) to be included in the town-wide sanitation and hygiene plan. The plan will be officially signed-off by the Town Council and harmonised with financial resources and investment plans, enabling framework; operation plans assumptions, risks and monitoring and evaluation plans.-

Phase 5: Learning and dissemination –This will be an on-going process, continuous through all four phases above. Learning notes and research papers; pathways to town-wide sanitation and conditions for scale-up. Published papers in academic / scientific journals would be an indicator of success of the contract with the academic research institution and consultant / mentor

1. Why?

 

Health Statistics in Rwanda reveal that most of diseases attended to at health clinics can be prevented though improved and inclusive hygiene and sanitation. In education domain, Rwanda’s vision on education is that “All Rwandan school children shall achieve their full development potential, by studying in  a  healthy environment  in  child-friendly  schools,  free  from  disease, prejudice and violence”.  The School Health Policy document puts emphasis on the “quality of life of the child in terms of healthy environment to take advantage of opportunities to thrive, so schools and communities can ensure that teaching programmes and school  facilities are  exploited  to their maximal potential”.  However, it is recognized that lack of adequate WASH facilities at schools continue to be a major hazard for school children with common water and sanitation related diseases including diarrhea, worms and typhoid.  It was found out that 66% of school children are infected with worms and 44% of pupils suffer from amoebiasis[1]. This project will contribute to the outcome related to the strengthening of cross sector collaboration for the sustainability of WASH.

 

2. What?

 

Project theme:

 

-          Provide inclusive sanitation facilities for healthy physical learning environment for school children

-          Increase access to safe drinking water in schools for the improvement of learning condition for school children

-          Increase hygiene practices and clean environment for school children especially girls.

-          Increase school-WASH governance and management capacity for teachers, parents, private contractors and education authorities

WARw will implement W4HL project according to WaterAid’s “School-WASH conceptual model” which, for sustainable and inclusive WASH, aims at ensuring quality implementation, sustainable management and adequate enabling environment through partnerships, capacity building and strong continuous analysis/learning. The framework of the already existing national programme for Environmental Health Promotion (CBEHPP) indicates a partnership and integration model needed for sustainable sanitation and hygiene services.  This programme is currently being implemented by ADEPR Nyamata Hospital, which is a District hospital under the Ministry of infrastructure, and it has been WaterAid’s partner since 2013 implementing the above-mentioned programme. It’s annual budget is estimated at 1.8 billion Rwanda francs and has 175 permanent staff. The projects implemented so far in partnership with WaterAid makes a total budget of 170 million Rwanda francs for a period of 3 years.

At community level, the W4HL project will synergize with Community Hygiene Clubs and the network of Community Health Workers operating at village and households level to ensure continuity of services for pupils when they leave the school environment.

1. Why?

 

The Government of Uganda Water Adaptation Strategy Report (GoU, 2011) notes the main economic sectors that are directly affected by water supply and variability include agriculture and livestock, fisheries and aquaculture, forestry and tourism. Complementary sectors include lake transport and energy production. Social sectors that are impacted include health and nutrition as well as water and sanitation. Seasonal and spatial variability of precipitation causes specific problems as the country encompasses both humid and semi-arid areas (MWE, 2014).  There are not only differences between distinct wet and dry years, but there are also considerable variations in the timing of the onset of seasons and in the amount of rainfall and hence stream flow. In particular, existing evidence suggests that droughts are becoming more frequent and more severe with major events occurring in 2001, 2002, 2005 and 2008 (UNDP, 2013). In Karamoja severe droughts are now occurring every two to three years as opposed to approximately every five years in the past (USAID, 2011). Future damages are difficult to predict as the agricultural sector faces two opposing trends. On one hand yields will decline for some crops due to climate change and on the other, the production will increase as a result of better techniques to manage inputs and investment in adaptive agriculture including water for production. Water is a key base for almost all human activity and the consequences of these changes are very significant throughout the economy and society but more so to the poor and vulnerable groups such as women and girls.

2. What?

 

WAU’s focus areas in this project will include the following:

1. Climate change knowledge for learning towards long term resilience

 

a)    Generating evidence on climate variability and its impacts on water resources and WASH services through research, vulnerability assessments and information dissemination at sub-national, national, regional and global levels.

b)    Promoting Integrated Water Resources Management (including underground water resources) as well as contingency planning for extreme floods and droughts

c)    Support institutional and human capacity building in water resource use, development and management

 

2. Strengthening the enabling environment for climate change resilience

ü  Strengthening climate change education, training and public awareness about existing options to build resilience and adaptation

ü  Performance monitoring about the level of action and inaction in implementation of the national climate change agenda spelt out in the Nationally Determined Contributions (NDC) and the climate change policy for national and global policy engagement

Estimated cost

The project is estimated to cost an average USD 100,000 per annum over the next 5 years totalling to USD 800,000. The first two years will cost above average estimated at USD 200,000 attributed to investment in research studies and vulnerability assessments, cost benefit analyses and designing of adaptive technologies and early warning systems. The funds to implement this project have not been secured.

 

Estimated timeframe

The project will be implemented over the whole strategy period of 60 months starting 2017 – 2021. This is meant to strengthen sustainability of WAU interventions through climate proofing.

 

Existing WaterAid experience

WaterAid will build on its experience in developing water for production designs such as sand dams in Karamoja to integrate climate change in water resources management for adaptation and community resilience. WAU will design gender-responsive climate change interventions that seek to protect human rights of vulnerable groups with a focus on women and girls

3. Where?

 

The project will be implemented in the cattle corridor of Uganda which stretches from North East to South West. The target population in this corridor largely depends on livestock and are greatly affected by climate change as a result of prolonged droughts which result into water stress and scarcity of pastures for the animals. 6 districts will be sampled from this corridor, based on vulnerability criteria that also promotes cross-learning and scale up of innovative resilience building technologies. Pastoralists are among the poorest Ugandans, with high rates of infant and maternal mortality, low levels of literacy, and limited political participation. They also are often poorly regarded by both government and their fellow citizens. Pastoralism is considered by many Ugandans to be a backward or declining livelihood with a limited future and headed toward a more or less inevitable transition to ranching, farming, or other alternative livelihoods. Nevertheless, the livestock sector contributes about 8 percent of GDP. Climate change has exacerbated resource scarcity and placed tremendous pressure on the pastoralist livelihoods and food security of the people in the cattle corridor, thereby increasing the potential for conflict over resources mainly water, land and pastures.

 

4. Who?

 

WaterAid will work with the Climate Change Department of the Ministry of Water and Environment, UN bodies such as FAO and UNDP currently implementing climate change response actions in the selected region as well as selected local and international civil society organisations such as ActionAid, WWF, Oxfam and Care International in Uganda. The climate action network Uganda will also be used as a platform for civic engagement at the national and global levels to pursue an inclusive climate change agenda for Uganda that observes the rights of women and girls

The stagnation in the WASH sector has worsened the situation especially on sanitation in informal settlements and in rural areas where 16% of the female-headed households are reported to be using the bush for toilets, compared to 10% of their male counterparts (UBOS, WASH Gender and water statistics profile, 2012). In all this, women and girls are the most affected because of their gender-associated vulnerabilities and inadequate civic expression and engagement. The Human Right to Water and Sanitation (HRWS) study (2015) indicates that in some areas where women are very strong they are actually active and success has been realised where the females are actually expressing themselves and providing leadership. However in some areas women still remain in numbers and still keep back in decision making. This project will support to amplify the untold story of women and girls to ensure that it reaches both the power and the political dimensions in Uganda to ensure increase in attention and prioritisation of women contribution to development

 

Her project is intended to build on the analysis, learning, empowerment and accountability aspects of strengthening voice. WAU has adopted a broader perspective of voice to encompasses all key state and non-state actors, including development partners, private sector, civil society, and citizens. Embedding the Human Rights based approach will be central to our work. WAU will profile and empower social movements and organisations that represent the interests of the poor and marginalized especially (women and girls) to appreciate and demand their rights to services from Government and service providers

 

The project approach will include activism for women rights to water and sanitation through empowerment and participation of women and girls in holding government accountable. This will be informed by WaterAid previous experience on improvement of WASH rights of women and girls Governance, Transparency and participation that looked at strengthening citizens capacity to demand for services, empowering Government to be responsive to citizens demands and the Citizens’ Action Projects which played a key role in enhancing users engagement as well as responsiveness of sector service providers.

 

Her project will enhance amplification of voices of women and girls who are the most affected because of their gender-associated vulnerabilities and inadequate civic expression and engagement. The programme will thus prioritise the protection of girls and women’s rights to water and sanitation as recognised in the 1995 constitution of the republic of Uganda. The provisions are not yet perceived from the social vulnerability aspects which impact on women and girls for instance the biological rights to birthing and menstruation which require special attention and services for women and girls. The gender division of roles leave women with the burden of managing challenges of water, sanitation and hygiene

 

2. What?

Through this project WaterAid Uganda will position herself as an advocate for WASH rights for the most marginalised people especially women and girls. This project will have a direct link to influencing balance in power and resourcing. This project will focus on empowerment, generation of evidence, strengthening involvement of women in development programmes.

 

For our advocacy to cause change, WAU will focus on generation of evidence and campaigns on WASH rights. Her project will play an important role in making clarity on existing narrative knowledge, and information. This will articulate the challenges faced by women and girls both at policy and service delivery levels and ensuring effectiveness of voice this project will equip rights holders (specifically women and girls) and duty bearers (government) with information on WASH rights, roles, responsibilities to demand government to fulfil its obligations while at the same time fulfilling their own responsibilities as users ensure sustainable WASH services. Information will be generated from existing data sources, research, academic and social learning forums and well as knowledge exchanges

This project will intervene on enhancing effective voices at different levels and ensuring this is impactful in making a case for WASH in the economic debate, and in institutional responses. User voices will be enhanced to articulate impacts at the household, school level, production level, public space and healthy facility level. The other interventions will include coordinating voices at different levels to effectively hold government accountable using participatory and social accountability tools such as community scorecards. At the local level, this will involve mobilisation of user groups/associations into either formal/structured social movements that are empowered to demand for WASH rights of women and girls. Similarly, non-state actors including the private sector, donors and media will be mobilised to catalyse the voices of marginalised groups. It will also be paramount that the lower levels of government (District Local Governments) are also mobilised for individual and collective voices as the situation may require. Engagement platforms will be identified or formed to provide space for dialogue.

 

WAU will facilitate citizen’s awareness Campaigns to enable them to understand their rights and obligations to, demand, pay for, utilize and sustain WASH services. The project will aim at raising the profile of WASH and use this to mobilize civil society to demand and hold Government and private service providers accountable on regulation and quality of services provided. WAU will focus on awareness on rights and obligations of the users through adequate analysis and information and promoting inclusive planning and decision-making processes

 

The project will implement activities geared at amplifying the voices of marginalised groups especially women and girls creating awareness on their right to WASH. An informed civil society, social movements and key influential individuals will be used as a channel to make informed claims that in turn will improve the responsiveness of government. The private sector’s voices on procurement, standards, and delivery of quality services will be enhanced to push for an enabling environment for private sector engagement. Amplification of donors’ voices will be informed by understanding the place of WASH, need for efficiency in proper utilization of donor funds, and visibility in targeting WASH as a driver of development.

The programme will further enhance the voices of districts, regions, line ministries, parliament and political party secretariats on issues of policy execution and regulation of the sector for better and equitable delivery of services.

 

Activities on building capacity of civil society and marginalised groups to participate in formal spaces of engagement will be prioritised. This project will target on increasing the visibility of grass root women in decision-making. This will be accomplished by increasing the capacity of women politicians at regional level to gather information analyse  proposed legislation, budgets and existing policies to ensure gender sensitiveness,  This particular intervention area is linked to the WAU Effective and Efficient Institutions Programme. User associations will be formed and/or strengthened to undertake independent monitoring of WASH service delivery systems and hold government to account. Similarly, other stakeholder of private sector, media, district local governments and parliament will be mobilised to also ensure a coordinated voices in demanding for WASH services.

WAU will work to strengthen existing platforms for civil society and marginalised groups to voices their demands. In situations where such platforms do not exist, WAU will facilitate processes for creating them. The advocacy will focus on lobbying for appropriate policies and programmes against global commitments signed by the Government of Uganda like the Sustainable Development Goals and targets, how they are interpreted, domesticated, and implemented.

 

The project will highlight the centrality and recognition of WASH in Economic development as well as sector efficiency through enhancement of voice for accountability.
